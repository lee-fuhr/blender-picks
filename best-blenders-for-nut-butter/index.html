Quick answer

For foolproof nut butter, the Vitamix 5200 is our top pick; its tall, narrow jar and powerful motor churn ultra-smooth butters without needing a tamper.

The Blendtec 575 is the choice if you want short, pre-programmed cycles and a wide jar, while the Vitamix E310 offers a smaller footprint and a lower price for occasional nut-butter batches.

Nut butter is a blender’s ultimate test of endurance and blade geometry. The high-fat, low-moisture paste of nuts creates enormous friction and heat, demanding a motor that won’t bog down and a jar design that keeps the thick mass moving toward the blades. Not every blender, even expensive ones, can handle it consistently without constant scraping or overheating. When shopping for a nut-butter champion, focus on three things: a powerful motor (at least 1,000 watts or 2 peak horsepower), a reasonably narrow jar that funnels the thick paste into the blade vortex, and a tamper if the jar is large or flat-bottomed. You also want a jar that’s easy to scrape clean, because nut butter clings. The three picks below have all earned strong reputations for tackling this demanding task based on owner feedback and expert reviews.

Frequently asked questions

What kind of blender can make nut butter?

A high-power blender, generally 1,200 watts or more, with a sturdy jar and ideally a tamper. Nut butter is a thick, low-moisture job that stalls weak motors and bogs down in the blades. A premium full-size blender is the reliable tool; most budget and personal blenders can’t do it without burning out.

Do I need a tamper to make nut butter?

It helps enormously. A tamper lets you push nuts down into the blades as the mixture thickens and stops the blender from jamming or air-pocketing. Some blenders manage nut butter without one using pulse and patience, but a tamper makes it far faster, smoother, and easier on the motor.

Will making nut butter damage my blender?

Not if the blender is rated for it and you work in short bursts to avoid overheating. Thick jobs draw high current and build friction heat, so let the motor rest between runs. Pushing a blender that isn’t built for dense work is the fastest way to kill the motor.

Why won’t my nuts turn into butter?

Usually too little volume, not enough power, or stopping too soon. Nuts need to pass through a crumbly, clumpy stage before their oils release into smooth butter, which takes several minutes. A small batch can’t reach the blades; add more nuts, use the tamper, and keep going through the dry stage.

Can I add oil to help make nut butter?

A small amount of a neutral oil can jump-start a stubborn batch and smooth the texture, especially with drier nuts like almonds. Purists skip it since nuts release their own oils with enough blending, but a teaspoon or two is a fine shortcut if your blender is struggling.
